The Love Story In 1612, Prince Khurram, later known as Shah Jahan, married Arjumand Banu Begum, a Persian princess, who became his favorite wife and was given the title Mumtaz Mahal. Their love was deep and inseparable, but tragedy struck when Mumtaz Mahal died in 1631 while giving birth to their 14th child. The Construction Overwhelmed by grief, Shah Jahan dedicated himself to building a magnificent tomb for his queen, an earthly paradise befitting her memory. Construction of the complex, which included the mausoleum, gardens, a mosque, and a gateway, began around 1632 and was completed in 1653. The project utilized white marble from Makrana and was adorned with precious gems and intricate floral and geometric carvings, a testament to the skilled craftsmanship of artisans from India, Persia, and beyond. A Symbol of Love The Taj Mahal is considered a symbol of undying love, a statement from Shah Jahan to his beloved soulmate. The story goes that Shah Jahan once planned to build a black marble tomb for himself as a twin to the Taj Mahal, but his son, Aurangzeb, imprisoned him, and these plans were abandoned.
